\section{OQ-4: Epifluorescence Beamline Design for D1}
\label{sec:oq4}

This section provides the first-pass design survey for OQ-4: the cryogenic
epifluorescence optical path required for the D1 NV bolometer prototype.
D1 requirements: collect NV$^-$ fluorescence in the 637--800\,nm band while
rejecting 532\,nm excitation, operating at cryogenic temperatures ($\sim$12\,K
demonstrator, 4\,K ultimate), compatible with SiPM readout.
This survey covers: (1) objective lens selection with confirmed specifications
from Attocube's LT-APO series (standard, LWD, and ULWD variants, all confirmed
from manufacturer web data), (2) dichroic and filter design with Semrock product
specifications, (3) collection efficiency analysis with total-internal-reflection
constraints, (4) solid immersion lens enhancement options (Hadden 2010), and
(5) SiPM detector characterization using the S14420 datasheet in hand.
Jacob makes all design decisions; NVB presents options with quantitative trade-offs.

\subsection{D1 Design Requirements}
\label{sec:oq4_requirements}

\subsubsection{D1 System Context}

The D1 NV bolometer demonstrator uses NbN thin film on sapphire ($T_c \approx
15.7$\,K) with NV-diamond readout. Jacob's current cryostat reaches
$\sim$11--12\,K. The optical readout chain for D1 must accomplish:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Deliver 532\,nm laser excitation to the NV-diamond chip.
  \item Collect NV$^-$ fluorescence (637--800\,nm phonon sideband).
  \item Reject 532\,nm laser scatter to OD\,$> 5$ before the SiPM detector.
  \item Operate without damaging in-cryostat optical components across
    room-temperature-to-12\,K thermal cycling.
\end{enumerate}

The NV center is excited at 532\,nm into the phonon sideband of the $^3E$
excited state.\cite{Rondin2014} % Rondin2014 p.4 Fig.1
The NV$^-$ emission spectrum consists of the zero-phonon line (ZPL) at
637\,nm and a phonon sideband extending to $\sim$800\,nm.\cite{Doherty2013}
% Doherty2013 p.18 Fig.6 (NV emission spectrum)
At cryogenic temperatures, the ZPL gains relative intensity as
phonon-assisted transitions are frozen out, but the sideband remains the
dominant emission channel.\cite{Doherty2013}
% Doherty2013 p.16 Sec.4.2 (temperature dependence of emission spectrum)

\subsubsection{Architecture Decision: In-Cryostat vs.\ External Objective}

\jacobdecision{The single most consequential architectural choice:
Is the objective mounted inside the cryostat vacuum space at cryogenic
temperature, or does it view the sample through a cryostat window from
outside? This determines every downstream specification.}

\textbf{Architecture A (in-cryostat objective)}: The objective sits at
cryogenic temperature inside the vacuum, focused on the NV-diamond chip.
Achieves maximum NA; no window transmission loss; no long working distance
required. Requires cryogenic-rated objective or proven low-temperature design.

\textbf{Architecture B (external objective through window)}: The objective
remains at room temperature outside the cryostat, viewing the sample through
an optical window. Allows any standard objective; easy focus adjustment.
NA is limited by window aperture and sample-to-window distance.

What is the clear aperture of the optical window and the sample-to-window

can be evaluated.}

\subsubsection{Architecture B: External Objective Through Cryostat Window}

An external (room-temperature) objective requires long working distance to
reach through the cryostat window to the sample. For a 25\,mm window at
50\,mm distance, the maximum achievable NA\,$\approx
\frac{12.5}{50} = 0.24$. The geometric collection efficiency scales as
$\eta \propto \mathrm{NA}^2$ for small NA, so Architecture B with NA\,$= 0.24$
yields $\sim$3$\times$ less collection than the Hogele aspheric (NA\,$= 0.55$).

\subsection{Dichroic Mirror and Filter Selection}
\label{sec:oq4_filters}

The filter chain has two stages:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Dichroic beamsplitter at 45$^\circ$:} reflects 532\,nm toward
    objective (and sample); transmits 637--800\,nm fluorescence toward detector.
  \item \textbf{Emission filter:} additional suppression of 532\,nm scatter
    and NV$^0$ emission (575\,nm ZPL) before the SiPM.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Dichroic Mirror: 532\,nm Reflect / 637$+$\,nm Transmit}

\textbf{Thorlabs DMLP650} is the standard starting point for ensemble NV
magnetometry.\cite{Barry2020}
% Barry2020 Sec. II.B p.6 (filter chain description for ensemble NV: DMLP + longpass)

\begin{itemize}
  \item Type: Longpass dichroic mirror, 1\,in.\ diameter (25.4\,mm),
    3.2\,mm thick, UV fused silica substrate \cite{ThorlabsDMLP650_web}
  \item Cut-on wavelength: 650\,nm \cite{ThorlabsDMLP650_web}
  \item Coating: ion-beam-sputtered, hard-coated; 40-20 scratch-dig \cite{ThorlabsDMLP650_web}
  \item Angle of incidence: 45$^\circ$ \cite{ThorlabsDMLP650_web}
  \item \textbf{Trade-off:} The 650\,nm cut-on excludes the NV$^-$ ZPL at
    637\,nm and the 637--650\,nm portion of the phonon sideband.

\subsubsection{Cryogenic Filter Performance Considerations}

Standard interference filters shift spectrally with temperature due to
contraction of the thin-film coating layers. The shift coefficient for
ion-beam-sputtered coatings is typically 0.01--0.02\,nm/K~\cite{AlluxtOpticalFilterThermalShift2026}.
% AlluxtOpticalFilterThermalShift2026: thermal coefficient = 2--5 pm/°C for hard-coated IBS filters.
For a 293\,K to 12\,K temperature change (Jacob's D1 demonstrator):
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\lambda \approx 0.015\,\frac{\text{nm}}{\text{K}} \times (293 - 12)\,\text{K}
    \approx 4.2\,\text{nm}
  \label{eq:oq4_filter_shift}
  % Derived quantity: midpoint of 0.01-0.02 nm/K range applied to 281 K delta.
\end{equation}

This shifts the DMLP650 cut-on from 650\,nm to $\sim$645.8\,nm, narrowing
the gap to the NV$^-$ ZPL at 637\,nm from 13\,nm to $\sim$8.8\,nm.

\subsection{Collection Efficiency Analysis}
\label{sec:oq4_efficiency}

\subsubsection{Geometric Collection Efficiency Formula}

For an isotropic point emitter in a homogeneous medium with refractive index $n$,
the fraction of emitted photons captured by a lens of numerical aperture
NA\,$= n\sin\theta$ is:\cite{Rondin2014}
% Rondin2014 Sec.2 p.5 (solid angle formula for collection efficiency)
\begin{equation}
  \eta_\text{geom} = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 - \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{\text{NA}}{n}\right)^2}\right)
  \label{eq:oq4_eta}
\end{equation}
where $\theta$ is the half-angle of the collection cone.

\subsubsection{Total Internal Reflection Limitation in Diamond}

NV centers in bulk diamond emit inside a medium with refractive index
$n_d \approx 2.4$. Total internal reflection (TIR) at the diamond--air
interface confines photons emitted at angles beyond the critical angle
$\theta_c = \arcsin(1/n_d) \approx 24.6^\circ$.

\textbf{Even with a NA\,$= 0.95$ air objective, only $\sim$4\% of NV-emitted
photons can be extracted through a planar (100)-oriented diamond
surface.}\cite{Hopper2018}
% Hopper2018 p.5 Sec.IV: "even assuming an air objective with NA = 0.95, a maximum fraction of only 4% of emitted photons can be extracted through a planar (100)-oriented surface"

This 4\% ceiling applies regardless of objective NA for air-side collection.

\subsubsection{Crystal Orientation Enhancement}

Using a $\{111\}$-cut diamond (as opposed to standard $\{100\}$-cut) aligns
one NV family axis normal to the surface, optimizing the dipole coupling to
the collection objective.\cite{Hopper2018}
% Hopper2018 p.6 Sec.IV.A

\textbf{Using $\{111\}$-cut NV diamond gives a 65\% improvement in collected
photons at no optical hardware cost.}\cite{Hopper2018}
% Hopper2018 p.6 Sec.IV.A (65% increase from {111} vs {100} orientation)

\jacobdecision{Can Jacob source or has Jacob's diamond already been
characterized for crystal orientation? $\{111\}$-cut diamonds are harder
to obtain but represent a free improvement in SNR.}

\subsubsection{Solid Immersion Lens Enhancement}

A solid immersion lens (SIL) placed between the objective and the diamond
sample eliminates the diamond--air TIR barrier. For a hemispherical SIL
made of diamond (or high-index glass matching diamond's refractive index),
the collection can increase by up to $n_d^2 = 5.76\times$ (hemispherical
SIL) or $n_d^3 = 13.8\times$ (Weierstrass/supersphere SIL).\cite{Hadden2010SIL}
% Hadden2010SIL p.1: "the theoretical NA of collection is increased by a value equal to the refractive index (nd = 2.42) of diamond"
% Hadden2010SIL p.2 FDTD: collection efficiencies 5.6% (planar), 29.8% (hemisphere).

\textbf{This is the highest-impact single improvement for D1 collection
efficiency.} Micro-fabricated hemispherical SILs etched directly into
the diamond surface can yield collection efficiencies approaching
$\sim$30\%, compared to $\sim$5\% without SIL.\cite{Hadden2010SIL}
% Hadden2010SIL p.2: FDTD simulation gives collection efficiencies 29.8% (hemispherical SIL) vs. 5.6% (planar surface).

\textbf{Total end-to-end fraction (rough estimate)}
  & $\mathbf{\sim 0.2\text{--}0.5\%}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

This 0.2--0.5\% end-to-end efficiency is characteristic of NV ensemble confocal
systems without SIL. For ensemble magnetometry, the high NV density compensates:
the total photon flux is $\dot{N} = \eta_\text{total} \times R_\text{sat} \times N_\text{NV}$
where $N_\text{NV}$ is the number of NV centers in the illuminated volume.\cite{Barry2020}
% Barry2020 Sec. II (ensemble photon flux scales with NV density and volume)

\subsection{SiPM vs.\ sCMOS for D1 Readout}
\label{sec:oq4_detector}

\subsubsection{SiPM: S14420-3050MG (Jacob's Device)}

Jacob's D1 system uses the Hamamatsu S14420-3050MG, characterized in OQ-6.
Key specifications from the S14420 datasheet:\cite{S14420datasheet}
% S14420datasheet: PDF in hand NVBolometer/hardware/sipm_fpga/references/S14420_datasheet.pdf
\begin{itemize}
  \item Peak PDE: 40\% at $\lambda = 600$\,nm (Vop\,$=$\,V$_\text{BR}$\,$+$\,5\,V)
    \cite{S14420datasheet}
  \item Spectral range: 350--1000\,nm \cite{S14420datasheet}
  \item Effective photosensitive area: $\phi\,3.0$\,mm \cite{S14420datasheet}
  \item Dark count rate: 1600\,kcps (typ., $T = 25^\circ$C) \cite{S14420datasheet}
  \item Gain: $3.6 \times 10^6$ (typ.) \cite{S14420datasheet}
  \item Breakdown voltage: $V_\text{BR} = 42$\,V (typ., $T = 25^\circ$C)
    \cite{S14420datasheet}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{PDE at NV wavelengths:} At 700\,nm the PDE is estimated at $\sim$25--30\%.\cite{S14420datasheet}
% S14420datasheet p.3 Fig. KAPDB0386EB "Photon detection efficiency vs. wavelength":
% Peak PDE = 40\% at 600 nm (S14420-**50MG, Vop = VBR + 5V, Ta = 25 degC).
% At 700 nm the 50 um pixel pitch curve reads approximately 25-30\%.
% Locator: S14420datasheet p.3 Fig. KAPDB0386EB.

\textbf{Cryogenic operation:} The S14420 is rated to $-40^\circ$C (233\,K)
per manufacturer specification but not to 12\,K.\cite{S14420datasheet}
Dark count rate drops dramatically below 77\,K, which is favorable for
NV fluorescence detection.\cite{SiPMcryoArrays2025}
% SiPMcryoArrays2025 (arXiv:2502.02235): SiPM dark count characterization 40-300K
The $V_\text{BR}$ shift from 42\,V (300\,K) to $\sim$28.6\,V (12\,K)
requires temperature compensation (see OQ-6 and
\texttt{hardware/sipm\_vbr\_temperature\_compensation.tex}).

\subsubsection{sCMOS: Widefield Imaging Option}

For D2/D3 multi-pixel imaging, a scientific CMOS (sCMOS) camera provides
simultaneous spatial readout of the NV ensemble.
\begin{itemize}
  \item Typical sCMOS quantum efficiency at 637--800\,nm: $\sim$70--80\%
    (e.g., Andor Zyla or Hamamatsu ORCA series).\needref{sCMOS\_QE\_spec}
  \item \textbf{Advantage:} Full widefield image of all pixels simultaneously;
    enables array operation for D2/D3.
  \item \textbf{Disadvantage for D1:} Requires widefield illumination (no
    scanning confocal); more complex readout; higher cost.
\end{itemize}

choice. sCMOS becomes essential for D2/D3 array readout. Does Jacob want D1
to demonstrate widefield imaging as a proof-of-concept for D2/D3?}
