1. Crystal Structure and the NV Defect

Diamond is face-centered cubic (FCC) with a two-atom basis. Each carbon is sp³-bonded to four nearest neighbors along the tetrahedral ⟨111⟩ directions. The lattice constant is a = 3.567 Å [Doherty 2013, §2.1].

The nitrogen-vacancy (NV) center is a point defect: one carbon atom replaced by nitrogen (N) adjacent to a vacant lattice site (V). The N-V axis lies along one of the four crystallographically equivalent ⟨111⟩ directions. The defect has C3v symmetry — 3-fold rotational symmetry about the N-V axis, plus three mirror planes containing that axis [Maze 2011, §II].

The relevant charge state for magnetometry is NV− (negatively charged), which has 6 electrons: 2 from the vacancy dangling bonds, 2 from the nitrogen, and 1 captured from the lattice. This gives a spin-1 (S=1) ground state [Doherty 2013, §3].

Why NV− and not NV0?

NV0 has spin-1/2 and no optically detected magnetic resonance (ODMR) contrast. NV− has spin-1 with a spin-dependent fluorescence mechanism that enables ODMR. The charge state can switch under illumination; typical NV experiments ensure stable NV− population via appropriate laser power and wavelength (532 nm) [Mittiga 2018].

2. Electronic Structure

The NV− ground state is a spin triplet: 3A2 (orbital singlet, spin S=1). The excited state is also a spin triplet: 3E (orbital doublet, spin S=1).

Key energy levels [Doherty 2013, §3; Maze 2011]:

The ODMR Mechanism

  1. Green laser (532 nm) excites both ms = 0 and ms = ±1 to 3E
  2. ms = 0 decays radiatively back to ms = 0 ground state (bright)
  3. ms = ±1 has a competing pathway through the singlet states (intersystem crossing, ISC) that is non-radiative (dark)
  4. The singlet pathway preferentially decays to ms = 0 (spin polarization)
  5. Net effect: ms = ±1 fluoresces ~30% less than ms = 0
Therefore: when microwaves drive the ms = 0 → ms = ±1 transition, fluorescence drops. This is the ODMR dip.

3. Ground-State Spin Hamiltonian

The full ground-state Hamiltonian for NV− in an external magnetic field is [Doherty 2013, Eq. 20; Maze 2011, Eq. 1; Rondin 2014, Eq. 1]:

$$\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{H}_\text{ZFS} + \mathcal{H}_\text{Zeeman} + \mathcal{H}_\text{hyperfine} + \mathcal{H}_\text{electric} + \mathcal{H}_\text{strain}$$

For ODMR magnetometry, the dominant terms are:

$$\mathcal{H} = D \, S_z^2 + E \left( S_x^2 - S_y^2 \right) + \gamma_e \, \mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{S}$$
The working Hamiltonian for ensemble CW-ODMR

where:

  • $\mathbf{S} = (S_x, S_y, S_z)$ are the spin-1 operators (3×3 matrices)
  • z-axis is along the NV symmetry axis (the N→V direction)
  • $D$ is the axial zero-field splitting parameter
  • $E$ is the transverse zero-field splitting (strain/electric field)
  • $\gamma_e$ is the NV gyromagnetic ratio = $g_e \mu_B / h$
  • $\mathbf{B} = (B_x, B_y, B_z)$ is the external magnetic field in the NV frame

Spin-1 Matrices

In the $\{|{+1}\rangle, |{0}\rangle, |{-1}\rangle\}$ basis (eigenstates of $S_z$):

$$S_x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \qquad S_y = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i & 0 \\ i & 0 & -i \\ 0 & i & 0 \end{pmatrix} \qquad S_z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Full Hamiltonian Matrix (in NV frame)

Writing $B_\parallel = B_z$ (along NV axis) and $B_\perp = \sqrt{B_x^2 + B_y^2}$:

$$\mathcal{H} = \begin{pmatrix} D + \gamma B_\parallel & \frac{\gamma B_\perp}{\sqrt{2}} & E \\ \frac{\gamma B_\perp}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 & \frac{\gamma B_\perp}{\sqrt{2}} \\ E & \frac{\gamma B_\perp}{\sqrt{2}} & D - \gamma B_\parallel \end{pmatrix}$$

The off-diagonal $E$ terms couple $|{+1}\rangle$ and $|{-1}\rangle$. The $B_\perp$ terms couple $|{0}\rangle$ to $|{\pm 1}\rangle$. When $E = 0$ and $B_\perp = 0$, the eigenvalues are trivially $D + \gamma B_\parallel$, 0, and $D - \gamma B_\parallel$.

Full Hamiltonian Including Electric/Strain Fields

For completeness, the full ground-state Hamiltonian including Stark and strain interactions is [Doherty 2013, Eq. 20]:

$$\mathcal{H}_\text{GS} = D \left[ S_z^2 - \tfrac{2}{3} \right] + \gamma_e \, \mathbf{S} \cdot \mathbf{B} + d_\parallel (\mathcal{E}_z + \delta_z) \left[ S_z^2 - \tfrac{2}{3} \right] + d_\perp (\mathcal{E}_x + \delta_x)(S_y^2 - S_x^2) + d_\perp (\mathcal{E}_y + \delta_y)(S_x S_y + S_y S_x)$$

where $\mathcal{E}$ is the electric field and $\delta$ is the strain field, with coupling constants $d_\parallel = 0.35$ Hz·cm/V and $d_\perp = 17$ Hz·cm/V. In practice, we absorb the strain/electric field effects into effective D and E parameters:

  • $D_\text{eff} = D + d_\parallel (\mathcal{E}_z + \delta_z)$ ← axial shift (tiny, negligible)
  • $E_\text{eff} = d_\perp \sqrt{(\mathcal{E}_x + \delta_x)^2 + (\mathcal{E}_y + \delta_y)^2}$ ← transverse splitting

4. Zero-Field Splitting: D and E

D — Axial Zero-Field Splitting

Physical origin: Spin-spin dipolar interaction between the two unpaired electrons in the 3A2 state. The NV axis provides an anisotropic environment: the two electrons' dipolar coupling is different along vs. perpendicular to the N-V axis [Doherty 2013, §3.2].

$$D = \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} (g_e \mu_B)^2 \left\langle \frac{1 - 3\cos^2\theta}{r^3} \right\rangle$$

where the average is over the two-electron wavefunction.

Temperature Dependence of D

D(T) decreases with increasing temperature. Physical origin: ~85% electron-phonon interaction, ~15% thermal lattice expansion [Doherty 2014].

Original measurement [Acosta 2010]:

$$\frac{dD}{dT} = -74.2(7) \text{ kHz/K} \qquad \text{(near room temperature, 280-330K)}$$

Modern best-practice model: Cambria 2023 two-phonon Bose-Einstein

$$D(T) = D_0 + c_1 \, n_1(T) + c_2 \, n_2(T)$$

where $\displaystyle n_i(T) = \frac{1}{e^{\Delta_i / k_B T} - 1}$ is the Bose-Einstein phonon occupation number.

At 12K: $n_1(12\text{K}) = 1/[e^{681/12}-1] \sim e^{-57} \approx 0$. Both phonon modes are exponentially frozen out. D is a constant. A 0.1K fluctuation shifts D by < 10 kHz — far below our 0.1 MHz bin size. This is why we work at cryo.

E — Transverse Zero-Field Splitting (Strain Parameter)

Physical origin: Breaks the C3v symmetry. Any perturbation that distinguishes $x$ from $y$ in the plane perpendicular to the NV axis:

  • Crystal strain (most common in bulk diamond)
  • Local electric fields (from nearby charges)
  • Surface proximity (for shallow NVs)

E lifts the degeneracy of $m_s = +1$ and $m_s = -1$ at zero field.

Diamond typeTypical E
High-quality CVD< 1 MHz
Natural diamond~1-10 MHz
Nanodiamonds~5-20 MHz

Effect on ODMR:

  • At zero field: two dips at $D + E$ and $D - E$ (splitting = $2E$)
  • At finite field ($\gamma B_\parallel \gg E$): enters as $\sqrt{(\gamma B_\parallel)^2 + E^2}$ — small correction

How to measure E: Zero-field ODMR. Two dips separated by $2E$. Mean gives $D$. If $E$ < linewidth, two dips merge.

5. Zeeman Interaction and Transition Frequencies

Decomposition of B in the NV frame

For a single NV orientation with axis $\hat{n}$:

$$B_\parallel = \mathbf{B} \cdot \hat{n} \qquad B_\perp = \sqrt{|\mathbf{B}|^2 - B_\parallel^2}$$

Eigenvalues of the Ground-State Hamiltonian

The 3×3 Hamiltonian matrix has three eigenvalues. The two ODMR-active transitions are $m_s = 0 \to \pm 1$.

Exact transition frequencies (for $E = 0$) [Rondin 2014, Eq. 3]:

$$f_\pm = D + \frac{(\gamma B_\perp)^2}{2D} \pm \gamma B_\parallel \sqrt{1 + \frac{(\gamma B_\perp)^2}{D^2 - (\gamma B_\parallel)^2}}$$

Exact inverse formulas [Rondin 2014] — Given measured $f_u$ and $f_l$:

$$|B| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3} \, \gamma} \sqrt{f_u^2 + f_l^2 - f_u f_l - D^2}$$ $$\cos^2\theta = \frac{-(f_u + f_l)^3 + 3f_u^3 + 3f_l^3}{27 D (\gamma B)^2} + \frac{2D^2}{27(\gamma B)^2} + \frac{1}{3}$$

Perturbative Result ($\gamma |B| \ll D$)

Always true for us: $\gamma \cdot 30\text{G} = 84$ MHz $\ll 2878$ MHz.

$$\boxed{f_{\pm} = D + \frac{\gamma^2 B_\perp^2}{2D} \pm \sqrt{(\gamma B_\parallel)^2 + E^2}}$$
THE KEY FITTING EQUATION [Doherty 2013, Eq. 22; Rondin 2014, §II.B]

6. The Four NV Axes — Peak Pairing and Geometry

The Four Orientations

In the diamond lattice, there are exactly four distinct ⟨111⟩ directions [Doherty 2013, §2.1]:

$$\hat{n}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(+1,+1,+1) \qquad \hat{n}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(+1,-1,-1)$$ $$\hat{n}_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(-1,+1,-1) \qquad \hat{n}_4 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(-1,-1,+1)$$

These are the four body diagonals of a cube. Each pair subtends the tetrahedral angle: $\arccos(-1/3) \approx 109.47°$.

Projections onto B-field

Given $\mathbf{B} = |B|(\sin\theta\cos\phi, \sin\theta\sin\phi, \cos\theta)$ in the crystal frame:

$$B_{\parallel,i} = |\mathbf{B} \cdot \hat{n}_i| \qquad B_{\perp,i} = \sqrt{|B|^2 - B_{\parallel,i}^2}$$

Key property: The four projections are constrained — they are NOT independent. Given $|B|$, $\theta$, and $\phi$, all four projections are determined.

ODMR Spectrum: How Many Peaks?

Each NV axis $i$ gives two transitions: $f_{+,i}$ and $f_{-,i}$. Four axes → up to 8 peaks total (4 pairs).

B directionDistinct projectionsVisible dips
$\mathbf{B} \parallel \langle100\rangle$1 (all equal)2 (all degenerate)
$\mathbf{B} \parallel \langle110\rangle$24 (2 degenerate pairs)
$\mathbf{B} \parallel \langle111\rangle$2 (1 aligned + 3 equivalent)4
General direction4 (all different)8

Peak Pairing Rules

Peaks come in pairs $(f_{+,i}, f_{-,i})$ symmetric about:

$$\text{center}_i = D + \frac{\gamma^2 B_{\perp,i}^2}{2D} \approx D \qquad \text{splitting}_i = 2\sqrt{(\gamma B_{\parallel,i})^2 + E^2}$$
  1. Each pair is approximately symmetric about $D$
  2. Pairs with larger splitting have their $f_-$ dip lower and $f_+$ dip higher
  3. Pair centers shift slightly upward with increasing $B_\perp$ (second-order)
  4. Sum rule: average of all 8 dip frequencies $\approx D$ (to first order)

When Do All 8 Peaks Resolve?

Condition: minimum inter-dip gap between adjacent peaks from different NV axes must exceed the FWHM.

FieldVisible peaksNotes
10 G~2Only outermost pair (n4) partially resolved from cluster
20 G~4-6Partially overlapping features
30 G8 (all resolved)Min gap ≈ 10 MHz ≈ FWHM
35+ G8Well-resolved with comfortable gaps
This is why we scan to high field: resolving all 8 peaks uniquely determines the B-field vector direction ($\theta$, $\phi$) in the crystal frame.

Vector Reconstruction from 4 Splittings

Given measured splittings $S_i = f_{+,i} - f_{-,i}$ for each NV axis:

$$B_{\parallel,i} = \frac{\sqrt{S_i^2/4 - E^2}}{\gamma}$$

Define signed projections $p_i = s_i \cdot B_{\parallel,i}$ where $s_i = \pm 1$:

$$B_x = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}(p_1 + p_2 - p_3 - p_4) \qquad B_y = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}(p_1 - p_2 + p_3 - p_4) \qquad B_z = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}(p_1 - p_2 - p_3 + p_4)$$

2-fold sign ambiguity ($\mathbf{B}$ and $-\mathbf{B}$) resolved by known magnet geometry.

7. ODMR Transition Frequencies — The Fitting Function

Complete Model for Ensemble ODMR

The observed spectrum is the sum of dips from all NV axes:

$$P(f) = P_0 \left[ 1 - \sum_{i=1}^{4} \sum_{s=\pm} C_{s,i} \cdot L(f,\; f_{s,i},\; w_i) \right]$$
where $f_{\pm,i} = D + \frac{\gamma^2 B_{\perp,i}^2}{2D} \pm \sqrt{(\gamma B_{\parallel,i})^2 + E^2}$
  • $P_0$ = baseline (off-resonance) power
  • $C_{s,i}$ = contrast of transition $s$ on axis $i$ (typically 1-10%)
  • $L(f, f_0, w)$ = lineshape function (Lorentzian, Gaussian, or Voigt)
  • $B_{\parallel,i} = \mathbf{B} \cdot \hat{n}_i$,   $B_{\perp,i} = \sqrt{|B|^2 - B_{\parallel,i}^2}$

Fitting Strategy (for this campaign)

Step 1: Zero-field measurement (Saturday)

  • Observe 2 dips at $D \pm E$
  • $D = (f_+ + f_-)/2$,   $E = (f_+ - f_-)/2$

Step 2: High-field measurement (30G+ data)

  • All 8 dips resolved. D, E fixed from Step 1.
  • Fit $\theta_B$ and $\phi_B$ to match 4 splitting patterns (over-determined: 4 splittings, 2 unknowns)

Step 3: Intermediate fields (10-28G data)

  • D, E, $\theta_B$, $\phi_B$ all known — verify Zeeman linearity
  • Fit per-axis linewidths and contrasts
  • Deviations → higher-order effects or field inhomogeneity

Step 4: Sensitivity characterization

  • $\delta B = \sigma_\text{noise} / (dP/df \cdot df/dB)$ at operating field
  • $df/dB = \gamma$ for the parallel projection of the most-aligned axis

8. Linewidth and FWHM

The observed ODMR linewidth has multiple contributions [Barry 2020, §III]:

Intrinsic Broadening Mechanisms

1. Inhomogeneous broadening (dominant in ensemble)

Different NV centers see slightly different local environments (strain, 13C, charge). Gaussian contribution. Typical: 1-10 MHz for natural 13C abundance (1.1%). Reduced to ~0.1 MHz in isotopically enriched 12C (single NV).

2. 14N Hyperfine

Each NV has 14N (I=1). Splits each $m_s$ transition into 3 lines separated by $A_\parallel \approx 2.14$ MHz. Unresolved in ensemble with FWHM > 5 MHz. Contributes ~4.3 MHz apparent broadening.

3. 13C Hyperfine

Natural abundance 13C nuclei. Nearest neighbor: ~13 MHz splitting. Ensemble: statistical distribution → Gaussian broadening ~1-3 MHz.

4. Power broadening

$\text{FWHM}_\text{power} = \sqrt{\text{FWHM}_0^2 + \Omega^2}$ where $\Omega$ is Rabi frequency. At +5 dBm: may contribute a few MHz. Trade-off with contrast.

5. Homogeneous broadening (T2-related)

Intrinsic $T_2$ ~ 1 ms in bulk → $1/(\pi T_2) \approx 0.3$ kHz — negligible. $T_2^*$ (ensemble): ~1-10 μs → ~30-300 kHz — still small vs. inhomogeneous.

Lineshape Model

  • Best model: Voigt profile (Lorentzian ⊗ Gaussian). Lorentzian: power broadening. Gaussian: strain, 13C, 14N.
  • Single Lorentzian: acceptable for most ensemble ODMR
  • Single Gaussian: sometimes better for strain-dominated broadening
  • Our data: start Lorentzian, try Gaussian, Voigt if wings don't fit
Our observed FWHM ≈ 10 MHz (from 10G filtered grand average). Consistent with ensemble NV in mm-scale diamond with natural 13C and moderate strain. Should NOT change significantly with applied field.

9. CW-ODMR Sensitivity

Shot-Noise Limited Sensitivity [Barry 2020, Eq. 17]

$$\eta_\text{CW} = \frac{\mathcal{P}}{\gamma_e} \cdot \frac{\Delta\nu}{C \sqrt{R}} \qquad [\text{T}/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}]$$

where $\mathcal{P} = \dfrac{4}{3\sqrt{3}} \approx 0.770$ (Lorentzian lineshape prefactor)

SymbolDefinition
$\Delta\nu$FWHM of the ODMR dip
$C$ODMR contrast (e.g. 0.05 for 5%)
$R$Photon detection rate (photons/s)
$\gamma_e$28.024 GHz/T

Sensitivity improves with: narrower linewidth, higher contrast, more photons ($\propto 1/\sqrt{R}$).

Comparison of Magnetometry Protocols

ProtocolCoherence limitBandwidthTypical sensitivity
CW-ODMR~1/linewidthDC (broadband)~nT/$\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$
Ramsey (DC)$T_2^*$DC (broadband)~460 fT/$\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$
Hahn Echo (AC)$T_2$Narrowband~210 fT/$\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$
CPMG/XY8 (AC)$T_2$ (extended)Tunable kHz-MHzBest narrowband

Key distinction: DC sensitivity limited by $T_2^*$; AC by $T_2$. Since $T_2 \gg T_2^*$, pulsed AC protocols achieve much better sensitivity. CW-ODMR (our method) is simplest, broadband DC.

Slope-Based Sensitivity (our experimental observable)

$$\eta = \frac{\sigma_\text{noise}}{\max|dP/df|} \qquad [\text{T}/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}]$$
Directly measured from data without needing photon rates

From our analysis script:

delta_f = wing_noise / max_slope      (frequency noise floor, MHz)
delta_B = delta_f / gamma_NV          (field noise floor, Gauss)

Bin-Limited Regime

When noise floor drops below frequency resolution:

$$\text{bin\_effect} = \max\left|\frac{dP}{df}\right| \times \text{bin\_size}$$
If $\sigma_\text{noise} < \text{bin\_effect}$, we are bin-limited: precision is limited by 0.1 MHz frequency step, not noise. This is always our target.

Sensitivity Scaling

$$\eta(N) = \frac{\eta(1)}{\sqrt{N}} \qquad \text{(white noise regime)}$$

Until drift onset (typically $N \approx 15$-30 pairs), after which $\eta$ plateaus.
